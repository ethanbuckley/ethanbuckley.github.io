How children with Down syndrome build vocabulary

A large pooled study of the words children understand, say, and sign as they grow — and what the numbers mean for expectations and support.

Exploratory study  ·  11 international datasets, ~588 children, ages 8 months–10 years  ·  Preliminary findings

We pooled parent-report vocabulary checklists from eleven studies and compared them with typically-developing children from the large public Wordbank dataset. Instead of single numbers, the models estimate a range for every growth trajectory — so each result comes with an honest measure of how certain we are.

What we found

Understanding comes before speaking

Children with Down syndrome understand far more words than they say, right through the early years, and the gap closes only slowly with age. A child who understands much more than they speak is following the typical pattern — not falling behind it.

That gap is wider than in other children — especially early on

At the same level of understanding, children with Down syndrome tend to say a smaller share of the words they know than typically-developing children do. The difference is largest when vocabulary is small and narrows as it grows. Some earlier, smaller studies suggested the pattern looked "typical" — the larger pooled data shows it does not.

It is a stretch, not just a delay — and speaking is affected most

Both understanding and speaking are delayed, and the delay grows as vocabulary grows. Spoken vocabulary lags furthest: by around age two, most children with Down syndrome say fewer words than the lowest-scoring tenth of typically-developing children.

Signing helps early — modestly

Counting signed words credits children with expressive vocabulary they already have, and narrows the gap somewhat in the two-to-three-year window. It genuinely helps, but the spoken-word gap stays large.

Why it matters

  • Sets realistic expectations. Comprehension leading production is normal and expected in Down syndrome.
  • Points support where the need is greatest. Expressive vocabulary is the widest gap — and signing can act as an early bridge.
  • Averages are not destinies. Children vary a great deal, and every estimate is reported as a range, not a fixed number.

What to keep in mind. These findings are exploratory and preliminary — they will sharpen as more data arrive and the models are finalised. The typically-developing comparison is strongest for younger ages (roughly 8–30 months). And while "understanding leads speaking" is clear across the whole group, the data at this scale could not yet establish it as a cause within individual children over time.
