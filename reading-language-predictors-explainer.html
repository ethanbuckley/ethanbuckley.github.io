What helps children with Down syndrome progress in reading and language

A careful re-analysis of a reading-and-language teaching trial — what the teaching improved, what it didn't, and how confident we can be.

Re-analysis of the RLI trial  ·  54 children, 4 assessments  ·  Preliminary findings

Children with Down syndrome almost always find reading and language harder — but good teaching can help. The open question is which skills respond, and for which children. We re-analysed a trial in which children received daily one-to-one reading-and-language teaching (some straight away, others after a waiting period), using statistics that report an honest range around every result rather than a single number. Because children were randomly assigned to start early or wait, the teaching's effects can be read as genuine cause and effect.

What we found

The teaching clearly improved decoding — letter sounds and word reading

These were the strongest, most reliable results: letter-sound and word-reading scores rose, confirmed by several independent ways of analysing the trial, and more teaching sessions meant more gain. The effect held even after accounting for family background and non-verbal ability.

It improved the specific words children were taught to say

Expressive vocabulary rose for the words that were actually taught — a real, if smaller, gain.

It did not lift broad vocabulary or grammar

General (untaught) vocabulary and grammar scores did not move. This isn't a failure — it's the shape of learning: skills close to what's taught improve, distant ones don't (yet). It matches the original trial's own conclusion.

Where children start matters more than how fast they grow

A child's non-verbal ability and starting scores strongly predict where they begin, but tell us much less about how quickly they then progress. These are patterns, not proven causes.

Why it matters

  • Focus teaching where it responds. Decoding, letter sounds, and specific target words show the clearest gains.
  • Expect near-transfer, not broad transfer. Children learn what they are taught directly, so broad goals need to be taught directly too.
  • Averages hide big individual differences. Every result is a range from one small study — a guide to expectations, not a guarantee for any one child.

What to keep in mind. This is a preliminary re-analysis of a single trial of 54 children, so estimates carry real uncertainty and await replication. The trustworthy cause-and-effect results are the teaching effects — children were randomly assigned, so those comparisons are fair. Findings about which children do better are associations, not proven causes.
