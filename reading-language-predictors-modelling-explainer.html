Predicting language & reading progress in Down syndrome: design and statistics

A two-step, causally-careful re-analysis of a randomised reading-and-language trial: gradient-boosting discovery feeding DAG-identified Bayesian models that separate a genuine intervention effect from ability-confounded associations.

DAG-identified Bayesian · gradient-boosting discovery → PyMC / NUTS · Beta-Binomial · RLI trial, n≈54 × 4 waves

The problem

Re-analysing a small randomised trial (54 children, four waves) to get per-child predictive value, not just an average effect. The hard part is that nearly every interesting association (one skill predicting another, ability predicting outcome) is confounded by a child's latent general ability. Only the randomised assignment is clean. The design is built to keep those two apart, and to put an honest uncertainty on every estimate.

Identification & model

Causal DAG (revised 2026-07, 20 nodes) with a latent general-ability node GA and hearing status HS
group IG  ⟂  GA  (by randomisation)   ⟹   ITT effect τ is causal
skill → skill / predictor paths open through GA   ⟹   association only
yi ~ BetaBinomial(Nout,  logit⁻¹(ηi),  κ);  smooth terms via HSGP
τ → posterior median, 95% CrI, P(τ>0), ROPE δ   // no p-values

The DAG is the causal spine. Randomisation d-separates the treatment group from latent ability, so the ITT effect is identified. Every non-randomised path stays confounded, and is reported only as an adjusted association. The rule throughout: only the randomised contrast is causal.

Two-layer method

  • Layer 1 · discoverGradient boosting (LightGBM, MAE), cross-validated GroupKFold by child; predictors ranked by permutation importance + SHAP, never hard-selected. Associational only.
  • Layer 2 · estimateBayesian PyMC: Beta-Binomial on bounded counts, HSGP smooth terms, NUTS via nutpie, PSIS-LOO comparison, behind a convergence gate (R̂ ≤ 1.01, ESS ≥ 400, ~0 divergences).

The model family

  • CausalITT (randomised effect τ per outcome) · difference-in-differences using the waitlist crossover · gain- & level-factor ANCOVA (only the randomised term).
  • AssociationMechanism (skill→skill dose) · mediation (g-formula NDE/NIE) · adjusted baseline-predictor, dose-response, and latent-ability terms. All GA-confounded.
  • DescriptiveLatent change-score & joint growth trajectories · horseshoe-regularised ranking cross-check · a correlated-domain measurement factor.

What the models show

The intervention effect on decoding holds up: letter-sound knowledge (τ ≈ +3.6 items, 95% CrI [1.3, 5.8]) and word reading (≈ +2.4 words [0.3, 4.5]) are credibly positive under four independent identification strategies (ITT, difference-in-differences, gain- and level-factor), survive SES and non-verbal-ability adjustment, and show a session dose-response. Taught vocabulary moves; standardised vocabulary and grammar are precise nulls. This is a transfer gradient that shrinks with distance from instruction, and it reproduces the original trial. Everything outside the randomised contrast (mechanisms, mediators, predictor rankings, ability associations) is reported as adjusted, ability-confounded association, never as a causal lever.

Rigour & status. At n≈54, point estimates are Type-M inflated, so results lead with the credible interval, not the point. Priors are weakly-informative, scale-calibrated, and independently audited; floored outcomes use a pre-specified off-floor rule; the full pipeline (discovery → DAG → estimation → diagnostics → report) is reproducible and was cross-checked by an independent audit. The integrated report is still being written: the substantive numbers are converged but preliminary, from a single cohort awaiting replication.
